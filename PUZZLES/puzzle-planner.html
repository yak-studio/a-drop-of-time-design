Puzzle Design

Puzzles act as gates — solve it, earn the reward. Every puzzle teaches or tests one mechanic. Difficulty scales with unlocked abilities. Discovery first, solution second.

Mechanic key
TAB SWAP TELEKINESIS INTERACT TRAVERSE OBSERVE Z-AXIS
Design rule: Every puzzle must have a moment of discovery before it has a solution. The player should feel the "oh" before they feel the "ah." If they can see the solution from the entrance, the puzzle is too readable. If they can't find a foothold after 60 seconds, it's too opaque. The sweet spot is: one surprising observation unlocks everything.
Act I — The Forest · Abilities: core combat, TAB swap
Act I · Puzzle 01 · Section 2
The Obelisks
Teaching attack, then teaching the swap
Tutorial
Setup
Four tall obelisk stones block the path. Each has carved runes on its face — glyphs that glow faintly when struck. The stones are ancient. They were not placed here to stop anyone. They were placed here for something else entirely, and the parents are using them without understanding what they mean.
Discovery moment
First obelisk has three runes — player hits it, one lights. Hits it again, two light. Third hit — all three glow, stone slowly sinks into the earth. Clear. Satisfying. Then: the second stone has no runes at all. Blank face. The Father stands in front of it. Nothing happens. What? — TAB to Mother. The runes are there on her plane. They only exist for her.
Solution steps
01
Hit obelisk 1 three times (Father or Mother — teaches attack chain)
02
Obelisk 2 is blank on Father's plane → swap to Mother → runes visible → hit three times
03
Obelisk 3 has runes on both planes but requires charge attack on one rune — teaches the second input
04
Obelisk 4 split — Father activates left runes, Mother right runes, requires one swap mid-solve
Who plays
BOTH — sequential
Mechanics
INTERACT TAB SWAP OBSERVE
⬇
All four stones sink. The path ahead opens — and a narrow natural archway is revealed that wasn't visible before. Simple. The world opens. You earned it.
Act I · Puzzle 02 · Section 4
The Frozen Gate
What exists on one plane can move on the other
Act I — Early
Setup
A glacial waterway — not crossable. On the Father's plane, a heavy stone wheel mechanism sits frozen in ice on the Z-axis wall (the background). On the Mother's plane, the same mechanism is free but has no wheel handle — just the socket. The wheel handle only exists on the Father's plane, embedded in ice above reach.
Discovery moment
Player notices the wheel socket on Mother's plane — incomplete. Swaps to Father — there's the handle, but the mechanism is frozen solid and he can't reach it anyway. But the socket on Mother's plane is at a different height. The planes don't share elevation here. What's inaccessible to Father is perfectly reachable to Mother — if only she had the handle.
Solution steps
01
Father climbs the Z-axis ice wall to reach the frozen wheel handle (traversal + observe)
02
Father breaks ice around the handle by attacking it (the ice shatters — satisfying)
03
Swap to Mother — the handle now exists on her plane at ground level (plane logic: objects unlocked on one appear available on the other)
04
Mother inserts handle into socket, turns the wheel (interact)
05
Bridge mechanism activates on both planes simultaneously
Who plays
BOTH — handoff
Mechanics
TAB SWAP TRAVERSE INTERACT Z-AXIS
🌉
The bridge doesn't just rise — it erupts from the glacial water. Ice explodes outward. Stone slabs crash upward one by one until a walkable span exists. Both parents see it happen simultaneously. Worth the solve.
Act I · Puzzle 03 · Section 5
The Rune Wall — Dodge & Block
The stones teach you to stop hitting and start listening
Act I — Mid
Setup
A second set of obelisks — the player expects to hit them like before. But these runes pulse with a faint red light and the stone surface repels strikes — attacking them does nothing, or worse, pushes the player back. The stone is protected. Something different is required.
Discovery moment
The runes on these stones show different glyphs — not the attack glyph from before. A curved shape (dodge) and a flat line (block). The player either recognises them from the tutorial stones, or experiments. When they dodge near a stone — one rune lights. When they block — another lights. The stones respond to defensive action.
Solution steps
01
Stone 1: dodge in front of it (directional dodge activates the glyph)
02
Stone 2: hold block while adjacent (sustained block fills the rune slowly)
03
Stone 3: parry timing — a small projectile fires from the stone, perfect block lights all three runes at once (teaches parry window)
04
Stone 4: requires swap — Mother dodges her side, Father blocks his, simultaneous
Who plays
BOTH — final stone
Mechanics
INTERACT TAB SWAP OBSERVE
⬇
Stones sink as before — but faster, more confident. The player has earned the same payoff a second time and it still feels good because they understand the system now.
Act I · Puzzle 04 · Section 7
The Ancient Door
Both planes must agree before the world moves
Act I — Late
Setup
A massive stone door — clearly old, clearly significant. The kind of thing that shouldn't move. On each plane, a large circular dial is set into the wall at Z-depth (the background face). Each dial has symbols. Neither dial does anything on its own. The door remains shut.
Discovery moment
The dials have overlapping symbols — some glyphs appear on both planes, some only on one. Rotating one dial causes the other to partially respond — a faint echo, as if the planes feel each other here. The player realises: both dials must show the same symbol at the same position simultaneously — but each is only controllable from its own plane. One parent sets their dial, swaps, sets the other, swaps back to confirm the first hasn't shifted.
Solution steps
01
Father rotates his dial — faint glow shows which symbol is "alive" on Mother's plane too
02
Swap to Mother, rotate her dial to match — but Father's dial has drifted slightly (time-decay mechanic — planes don't hold perfectly)
03
Swap back, micro-correct Father's dial — now both are aligned
04
Both dials lock simultaneously — a resonance sound — the door begins to move
Design note
The drift mechanic is the key idea here — the planes don't stay in sync on their own. This is the game's central metaphor made physical: you can't hold onto one without the other slipping. Keep drift subtle — frustrating if too aggressive.
Mechanics
TAB SWAP INTERACT Z-AXIS OBSERVE
🚪
The door doesn't open — it falls inward, slow and massive, raising dust on both planes at once. The scale of it is the payoff. Something ancient just moved because two people forced their broken worlds to agree.
Act I · Puzzle 05 · Section 9
The Warden's Threshold
A puzzle that is also a tone shift
Act I — Pre-Warden
Setup
A narrow, low passage into the Warden's space — the bottleneck before the confrontation. The passage is blocked by a heavy iron portcullis. There is no mechanism visible. No lever, no wheel, no dial. Just the gate. And silence.
Discovery moment
The gate exists on only one plane. On the other plane — the passage is open. But it's a crawl space on that plane, too low to walk through upright. The solution is: one parent goes through the open passage (crawling), emerges on the other side, and pulls a counterweight that raises the portcullis on the other plane. Except — there is no counterweight. The parent who went through has to hold a pressure plate with their body weight while the other walks through. Letting go closes the gate. You can only both be through if you time it.
Solution steps
01
Observe — gate on Father's plane, crawl passage open on Mother's
02
Mother crawls through (traversal — bottleneck compression, audio muffles)
03
Mother on other side finds pressure plate — steps on it, gate raises on Father's plane
04
Swap to Father, walk through the now-open gate quickly
05
Both through — gate drops behind. No going back.
Tonal purpose
This puzzle is deliberately simple. The Warden scene needs to land with full weight — don't tax the player cognitively before the most important dialogue in Act I. The puzzle's purpose is the bottleneck feeling and the no going back moment when the gate drops.
Mechanics
TAB SWAP TRAVERSE INTERACT
🔒
The gate drops behind them with a final clang. No puzzle music. No fanfare. Just the sound of a door closing and the Warden's space opening ahead. The silence after is the payoff.
Act II — The Moraine · New: Telekinesis (item), Self-Teleport
Act II · Puzzle 06 · Early
The Lever Across
Teaching telekinesis — the broken world as tool
Act II — Intro
Setup
A locked gate. The lever to open it sits in plain sight — on the other plane. You can see it through the fracture shimmer. It's right there. Completely unreachable by walking. A deliberate tease: the game is showing you the problem and the solution at the same time, and asking you to figure out the verb.
Discovery moment
The player tries to swap planes to reach the lever — but the gate is also on that plane, and it's locked too. Both planes, locked. Then — the player looks at the lever with the new telekinesis ability and reaches across. The lever disappears from the other plane and materialises here. The fracture is a delivery system. This is the moment the mechanic clicks.
Solution steps
01
See the lever on the other plane — can't reach by walking
02
Use telekinesis to pull lever across the plane boundary (first real use)
03
Lever appears on active plane — insert into socket, pull (interact)
04
Gate opens on both planes
Who plays
FATHER (intro)
Mechanics
TELEKINESIS INTERACT OBSERVE
🚪
Gate opens. Simple. But the player just learned that the boundary between planes is not just an obstacle — it's a channel. That realisation is the real payoff.
Act II · Puzzle 07 · Mid
The Counterweight
Weight on one plane, consequence on the other
Act II — Mid
Setup
A rope-and-pulley bridge spans a crevasse — but it's stuck. The counterweight on one plane is missing, replaced by an empty hook. On the other plane, a large ice block sits nearby — too heavy to carry, too far from the hook to place manually. The bridge sags uselessly.
Discovery moment
The ice block is on the wrong plane AND at the wrong position. Telekinesis can move it — but it can't cross the plane gap AND cover the lateral distance at the same time. Unless: one parent self-teleports to the far side of the block, telekineses it from that new angle, landing it precisely on the hook on the other plane. Position matters. The solution is getting to the right vantage point first.
Solution steps
01
Observe — ice block present but wrong plane, wrong position
02
Self-teleport to elevated position behind the ice block (positioning puzzle)
03
Telekinesis from new angle — arc carries ice block across plane boundary onto the hook
04
Weight on hook — bridge rises and locks
Who plays
MOTHER
Mechanics
TELEKINESIS SELF-TELEPORT OBSERVE
🌉
The rope snaps taut, the bridge swings up and locks into the cliff face. Solid. Walkable. Worth the positioning work to get there.
Act II · Puzzle 08 · Late
The Ice Lens
Light that only exists on one plane can thaw a door on both
Act II — Late
Setup
A thick wall of glacial ice seals the path. A single shaft of sunlight enters from above — but only on one plane. On that plane, a large curved ice lens sits on a ledge at Z-depth, angled wrong. Aimed correctly, the focused light would melt the seal. On the other plane — darkness. No light, no lens, just the ice wall.
Discovery moment
The player on the light-plane realises the lens needs to rotate — but it's on a high ledge and self-teleport only covers short range. Swapping to the other parent (dark plane) — they can see through the ice wall. The ice is translucent from the dark side, and the position of the focused beam is visible as a glow through the ice. The dark-plane parent can see where the beam is hitting but the light-plane parent can't see the target. They have to navigate together: light-plane moves the lens, dark-plane reads the result. One parent sees the tool. The other sees the effect.
Solution steps
01
Light-plane: telekinesis the lens from its ledge, hold it in the beam
02
Dark-plane: observe glow through the ice — the glow moves as lens moves. Guide it toward the seal.
03
Swap between parents to triangulate — one moves, other confirms position
04
Focused beam hits the seal — ice cracks, melts, fractures dramatically
Design note
This is the most emotionally resonant puzzle in Act II — one parent sees what the other can't. It's the marriage mechanic expressed as a puzzle. Build the "guidance" feeling carefully — it should feel like communication, not like guesswork.
Mechanics
TELEKINESIS TAB SWAP OBSERVE Z-AXIS
❄
The ice seal explodes outward in a cascade of light and water. Steam fills both planes for a moment. When it clears — the upper basin is visible for the first time. The summit is real. It's reachable.
Act II · Puzzle 09 · Graduation
The Twin Locks
Every verb. Both parents. One solution.
Act II — Graduation
Setup
The upper ridgeline is sealed by two enormous lock mechanisms — one per plane. Each lock has three components: a key stone (exists on the wrong plane), a dial (requires precise positioning from a ledge only reachable by self-teleport), and a pressure plate (requires sustained weight — so a telekinesis-held object, not a person). No single parent can solve their lock alone.
Solution overview
01
Father: telekinesis his key stone from Mother's plane across to his lock socket
02
Mother: self-teleport to elevated dial ledge, rotate dial to correct position
03
Father: telekinesis a loose boulder onto Mother's pressure plate (she's busy on the ledge) — holding it requires sustained concentration, locks Father in place
04
Mother: while Father holds the boulder — she sets her dial, self-teleports back down, pulls her lock handle
05
Father: releases boulder (plate depresses, triggers final mechanism), pulls his lock handle simultaneously
06
Both locks disengage — the ridgeline barrier falls
Mechanics
TELEKINESIS TAB SWAP SELF-TELEPORT INTERACT OBSERVE
⛰
The ridgeline barrier — a wall of stone that felt permanent — collapses in sections, each piece crashing down in sequence. The upper basin and the summit beyond are completely exposed. The parents stand at the threshold of the last climb. Everything after this is Act III.
Act III — The Death Zone · All abilities · No new verbs
Act III · Puzzle 10 · The Gauntlet
The Three Seals
Mastery test — no hand-holding, no time to think
Act III — Gauntlet
Setup
Three linked seals guard the path to the upper basin. They must be solved in sequence — but each solution destabilises something in the next. There is no pause between them. The mountain is actively hostile here. Wind buffets. Footing is poor. The puzzles themselves are not harder than before — but the conditions are.
Seal 1 — Familiar but pressured
A dial puzzle (like Act I's Ancient Door) — but the drift is faster. The cold is affecting the mechanism. Solve it quickly or restart. The skill is there. The patience is tested.
Seal 2 — Spatial inversion
A telekinesis puzzle where solving Seal 1 has shifted something — an object that was in one position is now in another. The player must re-read the space. Nothing is wrong. It just changed. Adapt.
Seal 3 — Both parents, no guidance
Final seal requires simultaneous action from both parents — but there's no preamble, no hint. The player applies everything they know. If they've been paying attention since Act I, this is satisfying. If they haven't — they figure it out. Either way, no new information is given.
Mechanics
TELEKINESIS TAB SWAP SELF-TELEPORT INTERACT Z-AXIS
🌬
The seals break. Not dramatically — quietly. Three cracks, three releases of pressure. The wind drops for exactly two seconds. Then resumes. The mountain acknowledged them. That's all.
Act III · Puzzle 11 · The Summit
The Convergence Gate
Not a puzzle. A choice dressed as one.
Act III — Summit
Setup
The Convergence. TAB now cycles three planes for the first time — Mother, Father, Daughter. The exit gate is open. One person can walk through. Whoever does, their plane collapses. The "puzzle" is: in what order do you go?
The mechanic
There is nothing to solve. No lever, no dial, no stone. Just the gate. Just TAB. Just the choice of who walks through first. The player has all the information they need. The Companion has explained the cost. The Uncle has offered his plane or not. The puzzle is the decision. Every other puzzle in the game was preparation for having the skill to make it. This one requires something else.
Why difficulty is 1/5
Because it's not hard to understand. It's hard to do. Those are different things. The game has earned the right to ask for the second kind.
Mechanics
TAB — 3 planes OBSERVE
∅
No fanfare. No animation. You walk through. The plane collapses behind you. That's the payoff. You made a choice and the world honoured it.
